When you search for something in The Bahamas — a local business, a government form, even a school — what do you find?

If you're like most people, the answer is: not much.  

A Facebook page last updated in 2021. A broken government link. Maybe a directory listing with no website attached.  

In one of the most connected countries in the Caribbean, it's surprisingly hard to find accurate, up-to-date, and locally hosted information. The Bahamas may be paradise in person, but online, it's a chain of invisible islands.



## **The Paradox of a Connected Country**

The Bahamas has no shortage of internet access.  

According to [DataReportal's 2025 Digital Bahamas Report](https://datareportal.com/reports/digital-2025-bahamas), more than **94% of Bahamians are online**, one of the highest rates in the region. Broadband speeds rival those of developed nations, averaging **over 70 Mbps**.  

So why, with so much connectivity, does our online presence feel so small?

The problem isn't that Bahamians aren't online — it's that Bahamian **content isn't**. 

Local businesses, schools, and agencies may have Facebook pages, but few maintain proper websites. And even when they do, those sites often exist only as a box-ticking exercise: *"We have a website,"* rather than *"We use our website."*

At Clever, we've audited hundreds of Bahamian websites. The same pattern repeats:  
- Broken contact forms.  
- Outdated information.  
- No SEO or local listings.  
- "Under construction" pages left untouched for years.  

They exist, but they're not *discoverable*. And if a site can't be found, it might as well not exist.

## **A Digital Desert in Paradise**

Search engines don't rank what they can't read.  

If a business only posts on social media, or its website hasn't been updated since 2017, search engines move on. That's why a Google search for "plumber in Nassau" or "Bahamas business licence renewal" so often leads to foreign sites, unofficial blogs, or unrelated tourism listings.

This invisibility affects more than private enterprise. Public institutions — from schools to local agencies — rely heavily on PDFs, Facebook posts, and word-of-mouth to share information. Many lack dedicated news sections, mobile-friendly layouts, or search-optimized pages that Google can index.

It's not that nothing is happening. It's that **none of it is being documented** in a way the internet understands.

## **How We Got Here**

A few forces combine to make The Bahamas nearly invisible online:

### 1. **Websites Built for Show, Not for Service**
Many businesses still see a website as a digital business card — something you "have," not something that *works*. These static sites rarely attract visitors or inquiries, because they weren't built to.

### 2. **Over-Reliance on Social Media**
Facebook and Instagram are useful, but they're *walled gardens.* Their content isn't fully indexed by Google, and posts age quickly. A business depending only on social media is effectively renting visibility on someone else's platform.

### 3. **Neglected Updates**
Websites built years ago without ongoing maintenance fall behind technically — outdated plugins, broken forms, missing SSL. That hurts both security and ranking.

### 4. **High Local Costs and Low Awareness**
At nearly $300–$400 USD per year, a .bs domain is among the most expensive in the region. That pushes most small businesses to generic .com domains or none at all, leaving fewer truly "Bahamian" websites online.

### 5. **Lack of Local Interlinking**
Websites rarely link to each other. Without those local backlinks, search engines can't map a "Bahamian web" as a connected ecosystem.



## **The Real-World Cost of Digital Invisibility**

This invisibility has real consequences:

- **Lost customers:** People searching online for a local supplier or service never find them.  
- **Eroded trust:** Outdated pages suggest neglect or unreliability.  
- **Missed efficiency:** Citizens spend hours hunting for official information that should be one click away.  
- **Lost opportunity:** Global attention — from investors to journalists — flows toward nations with a visible online footprint.  

When local information is absent, others fill the gap. Foreign platforms host our data, shape our narrative, and collect our clicks. Every missing website is a story untold — and revenue lost.
